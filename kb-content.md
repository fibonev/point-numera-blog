## 3. What Clients Actually Buy

Clients rarely come asking for "financial planning" or "FP&A". They come because they need clarity before a decision.

**Typical triggers for hardware/manufacturing clients:**
- Hiring a senior employee or expanding the team
- Expanding production capacity
- Unexplained cash pressure despite profitable trading
- Cost overruns with no clear visibility into where margin is going
- Preparing for external investment or financing (bank loan, strategic investor, equipment leasing)
- Hitting a growth plateau and not knowing why the numbers aren't following

**Typical triggers for restaurant clients:**
- Opening a second location
- Margins are shrinking but revenue is growing
- Cash is always tight and they don't know why
- Rising rent forcing a rethink of the whole model
- Menu is growing but profitability is unclear

Point Numera helps founders see the financial consequences of those decisions before committing.

## 6. Tone of Voice

### Personality
Grounded. Direct. Confident without being flashy. Operator-minded. Filip talks like someone who has actually run a P&L — not someone who has only analysed them from the outside.

### Core principles
- **Plain language over jargon.** "Your cash position" not "liquidity profile."
- **Concrete over vague.** Use numbers and specifics wherever possible.
- **Peer-to-peer, not consultant-to-client.** Talk with founders, not at them.
- **Credibility through experience.** Reference real operator background when relevant.
- **No hype.** No superlatives, no buzzwords, no corporate-speak.
- **Dutch directness.** Short sentences. Get to the point. No filler.

### Do say
- "Your margins are telling you something. Let's find out what."
- "Most operators don't know their real food cost until it's too late."
- "We work part-time inside your business, not outside it."
- "No hard sell, just clarity."

### Don't say
- "Leverage synergies" / "holistic approach" / "unlock value"
- "World-class" / "best-in-class" / "cutting-edge"
- "Solutions" as a standalone word — always specify what
- "Passionate about finance" — too generic

### Writing mechanics
- Short paragraphs: 2–3 sentences max
- Active voice throughout
- Oxford comma: yes
- Numbers: spell out one through nine, numerals for 10+
- Currency: €150,000 (€ prefix, no space)
- Em dash for mid-sentence breaks — like this
- Ellipsis: avoid unless intentional pause

## 7. Operator Perspective

Point Numera speaks from an operator's perspective. Filip has run a business himself. This should appear naturally in content — not as a credential statement, but as lived experience.

**Instead of:**
"Businesses should analyse their margins."
**Say:**
"When we looked at our menu, the dishes we sold the most were also the ones with the lowest margin."

**Instead of:**
"Companies should monitor working capital."
**Say:**
"Many businesses only realise they have a cash problem when payroll week arrives."

**Instead of:**
"Founders should build financial models before hiring."
**Say:**
"Before we hired our first full-time person, the numbers told us we couldn't afford it for another four months. We waited."

Content should feel like a founder sharing something learned from experience — not a consultant dispensing advice.

## 8. Founder Decision Lens

All content should reflect how founders actually make decisions. Founders rarely ask for financial analysis. They ask:

- Can we afford to hire this person?
- Why is cash always tight even though we're profitable?
- Can we open another location?
- Are we underpricing our product?
- Can the business support debt?
- Should we invest in this equipment?

Content should start from these real decisions and then explain what the numbers reveal. Never start from theory. Start from the decision a founder is trying to make.

## 9. Insight Structure for Posts

When writing LinkedIn posts or articles, follow this logic:

**1. Real situation** — start with a scenario founders recognise
**2. Hidden financial signal** — explain what the numbers are actually revealing
**3. Practical takeaway** — one insight the founder can use immediately

**Example:**
- Hook: "Most restaurants don't know their real menu margins."
- Observation: "The best-selling dish is often the least profitable."
- Insight: "Revenue growth hides margin problems."
- Takeaway: "Menu engineering should start with contribution margin, not popularity."

Keep the takeaway concrete and actionable. Avoid ending on a vague reflection.
